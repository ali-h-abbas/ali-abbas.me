In [[Plato]]'s philosophy, the form is the perfect, unchanging and eternal ideal that exists outside space and time. Not physical objects but the ideal of them, for example the idea of a "chair," from which all physical created chairs are imperfect reflections of this idea in the material world.

Rooted in the Platonic and Neoplatonic idea that the world as we see it is a mirror or reflection of a cognitive realm which both precedes this realm and is of a higher order. By extension of this realm where forms are perfect embodiments of concepts, the imitation of what exists in the corporeal world is one that strives for perfection but never quite makes it.

This striving forth, and what we strive forth is a key element of what defines our life's purpose and work.
