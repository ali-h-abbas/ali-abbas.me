Anicca represents the fundamental Buddhist insight that all conditioned existence is transient, arising and passing away in continuous flux. This principle, one of the [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Eastern/Buddhism/Three Marks of Existence]] alongside [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Eastern/Buddhism/dukkha]] (suffering) and [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Eastern/Buddhism/anatta]] (non-self), points to the inherent instability within every phenomenon - from the subtlest mental formation to the most seemingly solid physical object. The Pali term derives from "nicca" (permanent) with the negative prefix "a-", literally meaning "not permanent" or "inconstant."

At its core, anicca describes not merely that things end, but that [[30 -  Systems and Dynamics/change]] operates at every moment of existence. Buddhist contemplatives observe this truth through direct investigation of experience: [[20 - The Human System/21 - Somatics & Physiology/senses\|sensations]] arise and dissolve, thoughts emerge and fade, even apparently stable objects undergo molecular transformation. This recognition extends beyond simple observation of [[20 - The Human System/23 - Inner Architecture/Depth Psychology/death]] and decay to encompass the dynamic process underlying all [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Eastern/Buddhism/dependent origination]] - nothing exists in static isolation but rather manifests through ever-shifting [[causes]] and [[conditions]].

The contemplation of impermanence serves as both philosophical foundation and transformative practice within Buddhist [[meditation]]. Recognition of anicca naturally loosens attachment to what cannot be permanently grasped, while simultaneously revealing the possibility of liberation from suffering. This insight connects directly to [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Eastern/Buddhism/Sunyata]] (śūnyatā) in Mahayana thought, where the absence of permanent essence becomes a gateway to understanding the [[Middle Way]]. The principle finds parallel expression in Heraclitus's flux doctrine and [[30 -  Systems and Dynamics/Process Philosophy/Process Philosophy]], though Buddhism uniquely emphasizes its soteriological implications for ending [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Eastern/Vedic/samsara]].
