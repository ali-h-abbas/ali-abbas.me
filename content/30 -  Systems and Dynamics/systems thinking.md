Systems thinking represents the cognitive framework for understanding phenomena as interconnected wholes rather than isolated parts. At its core, this approach recognizes that the behavior of any system emerges from the relationships and feedback loops between its components, not merely from the components themselves. The whole exhibits [[30 -  Systems and Dynamics/emergence\|emergent properties]] that cannot be predicted by examining individual elements in isolation.

This mode of cognition arose formally in the mid-20th century through the work of biologists, mathematicians, and social scientists who recognized the limitations of [[30 -  Systems and Dynamics/reductionism]] in explaining complex phenomena. The approach draws from ancient philosophical traditions - from Buddhist [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Eastern/Buddhism/interdependence]] to Indigenous ecological wisdom - while incorporating modern insights from [[cybernetics]], [[chaos theory]], and [[network science]]. Systems thinking reveals how [[causality]] in complex systems is rarely linear; instead, effects loop back to become causes, creating patterns of [[self-organization]] and [[adaptation]].

The framework applies across scales and domains: from understanding [[ecosystems]] and [[organizational dynamics]] to parsing [[20 - The Human System/30-neurophysiology/Consciousness]] and [[social systems]]. It connects to [[30 -  Systems and Dynamics/holism]] in its emphasis on wholeness, to [[complexity science]] in its study of non-linear dynamics, and to [[design thinking]] in its application to problem-solving. Systems thinking serves as a meta-framework for navigating the [[interconnectedness]] that defines both natural and human-created worlds.
