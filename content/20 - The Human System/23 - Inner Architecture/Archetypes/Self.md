In [[20 - The Human System/23 - Inner Architecture/Jung/Jungian psychology]], the Self represents the totality of the [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Western/Neoplatonism/Psyche]] - both [[conscious]] and [[20 - The Human System/23 - Inner Architecture/Jung/unconscious]] - as well as the organising centre that guides the process of [[20 - The Human System/23 - Inner Architecture/Jung/individuation]]. Unlike the [[ego]], which constitutes the centre of conscious awareness and personal identity, the Self encompasses the whole of psychic life, including vast territories the ego can never fully illuminate or possess.

[[20 - The Human System/23 - Inner Architecture/Jung/Carl Jung]] distinguished the Self as the [[20 - The Human System/23 - Inner Architecture/Jung/Archetype]] of wholeness and integration - simultaneously the goal of psychological development and the guiding intelligence that orchestrates the journey toward it. This creates a paradox: the Self is both what we are moving toward and what moves us. It exists as potential from the beginning while requiring a lifetime to realise. The [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Alchemy/Philosopher's Stone]] of [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Alchemy/alchemy]] served Jung as a primary symbol for the Self - something both infinitely precious and found everywhere, the goal of the [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Alchemy/The Great Work\|magnum opus]] yet present from the start as the *prima materia* requiring transformation.

The Self manifests through [[dreams]] and [[active imagination]] as [[symbol\|symbols]] of totality: the [[mandala]], the divine child, the wise old figure, the union of opposites, geometric quaternity. These images compensate for the ego's inevitable one-sidedness, calling it toward a centre it does not command. Encounter with the Self carries a [[numinous]] quality - the sense of touching something sacred, transpersonal, and infinitely greater than one's familiar identity.

The relationship between Self and ego defines the task of [[20 - The Human System/23 - Inner Architecture/Jung/individuation]]: not the inflation of ego into Self (a dangerous identification) nor the dissolution of ego before Self (a premature [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Near & Middle Eastern Tradition/fana]]), but rather the establishment of an ego-Self axis - a conscious relationship in which ego serves as instrument of a greater wholeness it can acknowledge but never fully comprehend. This mirrors the [[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Universal Metaphysics/Perennial Philosophy]]'s teaching across traditions: that the small self discovers itself grounded in or identical with a greater Self ([[10 - Wisdom Traditions/Eastern/Vedic/Atman]], [[Buddha-nature]], the [[soul]]'s divine spark) - the "I" that seeks realising it participates in the "AM" of existence.
